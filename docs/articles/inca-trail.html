Backpacking the Inca Trail to Machu Picchu

by Katie · Posted June 14, 2002 · Cusco & the Sacred Valley, Peru
First proper look at the ruins after four days of walking.

I have wanted to do this hike since I saw a photo of Machu Picchu on a postcard in high school. Four days, three nights, and roughly two thousand stone steps later, I can confirm the postcard undersold it. This was the first really big trip of my life and I cried at the end, partly from the view and partly from my knees.

Day 1 – the easy lie

They call the first day "Inca flat," which is a Peruvian joke meaning "gently uphill forever." We started near Ollantaytambo and walked along the Urubamba river. Easy enough that I got cocky. Our porters, carrying triple my weight, jogged past me singing.

Day 2 – Dead Woman's Pass

The famous one. We climbed to 4,215 metres (13,800 feet) at a pass with a frankly rude name. The air up there is thin and I learned what altitude really feels like — a topic I'd revisit on a much later trip. Going down the other side was brutal on the knees but the cloud forest started and everything turned green and dripping.

Day 3 – ruins all day

My favorite day. We passed half a dozen smaller Inca sites perched on impossible ledges, with terraces and tunnels and stairs cut straight into the rock. Hardly anyone talks about these because the big one steals the show, but I'd have been happy if the trip ended here.

Day 4 – the Sun Gate at dawn

Up at 3:30am to line up at a checkpoint in the dark. We hiked the last stretch by headlamp and reached Inti Punku, the Sun Gate, just as the light came over the ridge and Machu Picchu appeared below in the mist. Nobody said anything for about five minutes.

Katie's tips: Book your permit MONTHS ahead — they cap the number of hikers. Tip your porters and cook, they earn it ten times over. Bring coca tea or sweets for the altitude, and break in your boots before you fly. Trust me on the boots.

If you only do one big hike in your life, I'd point you here. Just respect the altitude, respect the porters, and bring more socks than you think you need.
