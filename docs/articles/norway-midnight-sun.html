Camping Under Norway's Midnight Sun

by Katie · Posted June 2, 2004 · Lofoten Islands, Norway
Taken at roughly midnight. The sun just... hangs there.

Norway broke my brain in the best way. I went up to the Lofoten Islands in June, above the Arctic Circle, where in summer the sun simply does not set. I'd read about the midnight sun but nothing prepares you for pitching your tent in what feels like late afternoon and realizing it's 1:00 in the morning.

Losing track of time completely

Without darkness to tell your body what to do, the days turn elastic. I'd start a hike after dinner, summit a ridge at 11pm in full golden light, and wander back to camp with zero sense of how long I'd been out. I ate breakfast at 3pm twice. My watch became a purely theoretical instrument.

The freedom to roam

Norway has "allemansretten," the right to roam, which means you can legally wild-camp almost anywhere as long as you're respectful and away from houses. For a broke traveller in an eye-wateringly expensive country, free camping in spots like this is a gift. I pitched on a beach of white sand below jagged green peaks and paid exactly nothing.

Katie's tips: Bring a proper eye mask — you will not sleep otherwise, and a good sleeping bag, because "summer" up there still gets cold at "night." Stock up on groceries in bigger towns; tiny island shops are pricey and shut early.

Worth the splurge to get there

Norway is not a cheap country and getting to Lofoten took some doing — long buses, a ferry, careful budgeting elsewhere to afford it. But standing on that beach at midnight with the sun glowing on the water and not another soul around, I understood why people save for years to come here. Some places earn the splurge. This one did.
