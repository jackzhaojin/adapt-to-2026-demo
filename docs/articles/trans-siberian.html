6 Days on the Trans-Siberian Railway

by Katie · Posted September 28, 2003 · Moscow → Irkutsk, Russia
Hour 40-something. The view did not change. I loved it.

The Trans-Siberian isn't really a train trip, it's a way of life that happens to be on rails. I rode third class ("platskart," the open dorm carriage) from Moscow toward Lake Baikal, and those six days are some of the best I've had anywhere. You don't go to see things out the window. You go to live in a tin tube full of strangers who become friends.

Life in platskart

Third class is one long open carriage of bunks, no compartments, no privacy, and total community. Within an hour everyone has shared food. By day two I was playing endless card games with a family from Yekaterinburg, none of whom spoke English, all of whom adopted me.

The samovar rules everything

At the end of every carriage is a coal-fired boiler of endless hot water, the samovar, and your whole day organizes itself around it: tea, instant noodles, more tea. Here's how my time basically broke down:

ActivityHours/day
Looking out the window5
Card games & charades4
Tea & noodles at the samovar3
Reading / dozing6
Buying food on the platform1
Katie's tips: Bring more food than you think, plus things to share — sharing is the whole social currency. Babushkas on the platforms sell hot potatoes and dumplings; have small ruble notes ready. And a deck of cards is worth more than a phrasebook.

Time stops meaning much out there. The clocks on the train stay on Moscow time the whole way, so you genuinely lose track. By the time we pulled into Irkutsk and I saw Baikal, I was almost sorry the rocking and the tea and the card games had to end. Take the slow train.
